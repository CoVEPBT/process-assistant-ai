## Getting Started

### What exactly is a process?
A process is a series of steps that turn an input into an output to achieve a specific outcome. It describes what happens (the steps and decisions), who does it (which role), and the order they happen in. A process is broader than a procedure — the process is the overall flow; the procedure is the step-by-step detail for one task within it.

### What's the difference between a process, a procedure, and a work instruction?
A **process** is the high-level view of how related activities flow together to produce an outcome — the "big picture" of who does what, in what order. A **procedure** is the step-by-step description of how to perform one task within that process. A **work instruction** is the most detailed level, with explicit click-by-click or tool-specific instructions for carrying out a single task — often with screenshots, system names, and safety or accuracy checks.

### Why do we need to document a process?
Documenting a process makes it repeatable, transferable and improvable. New staff get up to speed quickly, existing staff have a reference, and you can spot waste, risk or unclear handovers. At RMIT, documented processes also support compliance, audit and consistent service quality across the College.

### Where do I start?
Start with the basics: the trigger (what kicks the process off), inputs (what's needed), outputs (what gets produced), and the owner/expert roles. Then map the high-level activities — 3 to 7 of them — before drilling into tasks. Seeing the overall flow first stops the process getting overcomplicated.

### Who should be involved in documenting the process?
The Process Expert is the day-to-day subject matter expert and usually writes the first draft. The Process Owner reviews and approves. Anyone else who performs steps in the process should review for accuracy. A working group of 2-4 people is usually enough to capture the detail without slowing the actual work down.

### How long should process documentation take?
A small, well-understood process can be drafted in 1-2 hours. A complex cross-team process usually needs a few short workshops plus revision time — typically 2-4 weeks elapsed with maybe 4-8 hours of actual writing. If it's taking much longer, the scope is probably too big and the process should be split into sub-processes.

## Scope & Boundaries

### What should be included in the process?
Include what happens 80% of the time — the normal flow. Capture each activity, the role responsible, and the tasks under it. Include decisions (Yes/No questions), handover points between roles, and the trigger, inputs and outputs.
Want an example?

### What should be left out?
Leave out the 20% of variations and exceptions — those belong in NOTEs attached to the relevant activity, not in the main flow. Also leave out detailed system click-throughs (e.g. "click the blue button, then the dropdown") — link to a work instruction or guide instead. The main process should read at a high level.

### How do I define the start and end points?
Define the trigger (what kicks the process off, e.g. "Staff member submits a leave request") and the outputs (what the process produces, e.g. "Approved leave recorded against the staff member's profile"). Be specific. "Process starts" isn't a trigger; "Staff member submits a request via Workday" is.

### Should exceptions and edge cases be included?
Yes — but in NOTEs, not the main flow. NOTEs use a question-and-answer format: the title is a question ("What if the form is incomplete?"), the body is the answer ("Send it back to the requester with a comment explaining what's missing"). This keeps the main process clean while still capturing the knowledge.

### How many steps is too many?
Aim for 3-7 high-level activities per process and around 10 tasks per activity (maximum). If a process has more than 10 activities, split it into sub-processes. If an activity has more than 10 tasks, the activity is probably doing too much.

## Roles & Responsibilities

### Who owns the process?
The Process Owner is accountable for the process being effective and improving over time. Usually a manager or team lead with authority to approve changes. One named individual, not a team or distribution list. The Owner doesn't have to do the day-to-day work — that's the Expert's role.

### What's the difference between a process owner and a process expert?
The Owner is accountable for the process being effective and improving over time — usually a manager or team lead. The Expert is the day-to-day subject matter expert who knows the detail and can answer questions. At RMIT they must be different people so the approval chain works correctly.

### Who approves the process?
In Nintex Process Manager, the Process Expert and Process Owner approve new and updated processes. Once approved, the process is submitted for publishing. A Process Champion or the Projects and Business Transformation team can then publish the final version. For significant changes (new activities, role changes, system changes), the Owner should also consult affected teams before approving.

### Who is responsible for keeping it updated?
The Process Expert keeps the content current — flagging changes when systems update, roles shift, or steps change. The Process Owner reviews and approves the updates. Owners and Experts are notified by Nintex Process Manager of upcoming or overdue reviews — the standard review cycle is every 12 months at RMIT.

### Should I use a flowchart, procedure, checklist, or all three?
Usually at least two: a process map (the visual flow) and a procedure (the written detail). Checklists are useful as attachments when a task has many sequential steps staff need to tick off in order.

### How do I write steps clearly?
Keep sentences under 20 words and start every step with an action verb. Each task should be a single, short, imperative clause — break compound sentences using "and", "which", "so that" into two shorter ones.

### What level of language should I use?
Write for a competent person joining the team — assume they understand the domain but not your specific tools or shortcuts. Use Australian English (organise, customise, centre, colour). Spell out acronyms on first use. Avoid jargon and filler words.

**I auto-correct American spellings to Australian and flag banned terms (TBD, TBA, "etc.") automatically.** Paste your draft into the *Give me your draft* panel and click *Analyse content* — I'll flag any language issues for review.

### How do I avoid making the process too technical?
Keep the main flow at the "what" level, not the "how" level — leave click-by-click system detail to attached work instructions. Use approved action verbs (Receive, Review, Approve, Notify, Close) rather than system jargon. If a task description needs more than 25 words, it's probably trying to capture work-instruction detail.

### Should I document the current process or the ideal future process?
Start with the current process — the "as-is". You can't improve what you can't see. Once that's accurately captured, you can run a separate exercise to design the "to-be" state. ## Quality & Consistency

### How do I know if the process is "good enough"?
I'll identify opportunities to enhance your process so it's high-quality. The opportunities you see after analysis cover the CoVE Golden Rules, RMIT standards, and Nintex techniques — when you've addressed those, the process is publish-ready.

### How do I make documentation consistent across teams?
Use me to export documents in a consistent format. I enforce verb-first naming, AU English, the Nintex structure, and role-based activities, so processes from different teams read the same way.

### What are common mistakes in process documentation?
The most common ones: writing tasks in passive voice or noun-first, lumping multiple actions into one task, using "ALL STAFF" or individual names instead of role titles, missing the trigger or outputs, and putting exception-handling in the main flow instead of NOTEs. I flag all of these automatically.

### How do I make processes easier to follow?
Keep activity titles short (2-7 words) and verb-first. Use 3-7 high-level activities so the reader can see the shape at a glance. Put exceptions in NOTEs, not the main flow. Use approved action verbs so every step reads as a clear instruction.

### When do I use decision points?
Use a decision (Yes/No question) when the process genuinely branches based on a condition — e.g. "Has approval been granted?". The Yes path continues; the No path should usually lead to one follow-up action and then end. Avoid more than two decisions in a row — if you have a decision tree, the content is probably better as a knowledge article than a process.
Want an example?

### How do I handle loops or repeated steps?
True loops are rare in business processes — most "loops" are actually rework after a No decision. If a step really does repeat (e.g. "retry up to 3 times"), capture it in a NOTE attached to that step rather than drawing a loop in the diagram. Keeping the main flow linear makes it easier to read.

## Improvement & Maintenance

### What if the process changes frequently?
If a process changes more than every 6 months, check whether you've captured the right level of detail — frequent change usually means the process has system-specific clicks or task-level detail that belongs in a work instruction. Keep the process at the "what" level (which is stable) and put the volatile "how" detail in linked guides.

### How often should processes be reviewed?
Default to a 12-month review cadence. Trigger an out-of-cycle review whenever a system, role, or compliance requirement changes, or when staff feedback shows the process is unclear or outdated. Note the review date in Nintex so it's visible.

### How do I identify improvement opportunities while documenting?
Watch for: long tasks that read like work-instruction detail, NOTEs that explain the same exception more than once (sign of a missing decision), handovers without a clear notification, or activities nobody can name a single role for. These are common improvement candidates.

### Should pain points and risks be documented too?
Yes — but as separate notes for the Process Owner, not in the published process itself. The published process documents how the work happens; pain points and risks inform improvement and risk-management activity. Some teams keep a separate "process health" register alongside the Nintex record.

## User Adoption

### How do I get staff to actually use documented processes?
A few options that teams have found useful: making the process easy to find (linked from Teams channels, dashboards, or intake forms), keeping it short and skimmable so people can scan it quickly, and reviewing it whenever staff flag that something has changed. Some teams also build it into onboarding so new starters use the documented process from day one. Assigning tasks to specific roles (rather than "ALL STAFF") helps each person see what applies to them.

### What if different teams do the process differently?
First check whether the difference is intentional (different volumes, systems, or regulatory contexts) or unintentional drift. If intentional, one option is to document a shared parent process with team-specific sub-processes for the variations. If it's drift, the affected teams may want to discuss whether to align on one approach before documenting.

### How do I make documentation engaging instead of bureaucratic?
A few things that tend to help: using plain Australian English rather than formal compliance language, keeping sentences short, and letting the structure do the work — readers should be able to skim activity titles and follow the flow without reading every word. Avoiding filler phrases ("In order to…", "It should be noted that…") and spelling out acronyms on first use also makes the content easier to read.
