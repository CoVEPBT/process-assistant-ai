CoVE Process Writing - Golden Rules

Eleven rules from the College of Vocational Education Projects and Business Transformation team for clear, consistent process maps.

  1. Define your trigger, inputs, and outputs. Clarifies when the process starts, what is needed, and what the end result looks like. Example: Trigger: Staff member requests a new laptop via ServiceNow. Input: Completed Facilities Request Form. Output: Laptop provided to staff member.
  2. Map high-level activities before adding detailed steps. See the overall flow start to finish before diving into details, to prevent overcomplicating the process and ensure logical sequence.
  3. Assign one responsible role per activity. Ensures clear ownership. Each activity should have one accountable role even if others contribute. Example: The Program Manager is responsible while the Quality Lead may support.
  4. Map what happens most of the time (the 80%). Keeps the process simple and relevant. Use NOTEs for the 20% of variations or exceptions.
  5. Keep language concise and to the point. Easy to read, fewer misunderstandings. Avoid filler words, long sentences, and unnecessary acronyms.
  6. Start each activity with a verb. Makes actions easy to identify and follow. Example: Receive request → Review request → Approve request → Notify staff → Close request.
  7. Use decision boxes for Yes/No questions. Decision boxes show where a process branches. The "No" path should lead to one follow-up action before ending. Example: "Has approval been granted?" Yes → Action request. No → Notify requester → End.
  8. Attach supporting guides or links. Helps users access additional information without cluttering the main process. Use "web link" only for internal systems or external websites.
  9. Done is better than perfect. Capture 80% of a process now and refine through feedback later, rather than delaying for perfection.
  10. Request a peer review. A fresh perspective helps identify gaps or assumptions you may have missed. Ask a colleague from another team to walk through the process map.
  11. Define clear handover points. Clear start and end points for each activity ensure smooth transitions between roles. Example: Activity ends when the "Request approved" email is sent; the next activity begins when the Facilities team receives that notification.

Source: CoVE Process Writing - Golden Rules (CoVE Projects and Business Transformation, November 2025).

RMIT Tips for Process Editors & Champions

RMIT’s practical guidance on how to write processes consistently across the University.

Writing style

  • Aim for clear, concise language at an acceptable standard for internal RMIT communications.
  • Use the RMIT Writing Style Guide as a reference.
  • Use verbs from the categorised verb list for tasks, activities and titles.

Process titles

  • Process titles should start with a verb.
  • In large organisations, name titles consistently - especially when there are several sub-processes within a life cycle.

Right-sizing your process

  • If you end up with more than ten activities, you may have two or more separate processes.
  • Look for a logical break or grouping to divide the larger process into sub-processes.

Activities

  • Aim for no more than 10 activities per process.
  • Aim for 2–7 words in an activity title.
  • Activities are the high-level steps - the “what”.
  • Activities must always start with a verb.

Tasks

  • Tasks must always start with a verb.
  • Avoid pitching at a work-instruction level - use attached guides instead.

NOTEs

  • Use NOTEs for the 20% of cases that differ from the normal flow (the 80/20 rule).
  • A NOTE is always a question and an answer. Title a NOTE as a question so the reader can skip if they know the answer.
  • NOTEs can also explain business rules, what-ifs, or background.

Owner and Expert

  • The Process Owner and Process Expert must be different people, so the correct approval chain applies.

Audience terminology

  • Use Users when referring to people interacting with a system (e.g. SAP).
  • Use Employees when an activity could be performed by any RMIT staff (e.g. report a Health & Safety incident).
  • Use Requestor for anyone requesting a service.
  • Avoid ALL STAFF - the process will appear on every Promapp dashboard.

Source: Tips for Process Editors & Champions, RMIT - Nintex Process Manager guidance.

Nintex Process Writing Techniques

Eight techniques from Nintex for writing clear, useful processes in Nintex Process Manager.

  1. Distinguish the normal process from exceptions. Document the steps that normally occur (typically 80% of the time). Use NOTEs to describe variations or exceptions. NOTE titles should always be a question.
  2. Recognise when multiple normal processes exist. Create separate processes when you have multiple ways of performing the same process. Example: split “Recruit Staff” into Hire Temps, Hire Contractors, Hire Full-Time Staff.
  3. Group common tasks into activities. Group tasks to identify the major activities. Limit to 10 activities per process. Tasks performed at the same time or by the same role can be grouped.
  4. Break large processes into sub-processes. Break long processes into smaller, more manageable sub-processes. Example: split “Recruit New Staff Member” into Obtain Approval to Recruit, Advertise Position, Select Candidate.
  5. Apply the verb-first rule. Start all process names, activity and task descriptions with a verb. Test by asking: “What is the person expected to do?”
  6. Use ‘if statements’ for conditional steps. Use “if required”, “if needed”, “if appropriate” to identify activities or tasks not always performed. Supplement with a NOTE explaining when.
  7. Use guides to capture transactional detail. Use documents/guides to reduce complexity. Detailed how-to-use-an-application instructions are useful when learning the job but rarely referred to once learnt. Attach the guide to the relevant activity step.
  8. Give the right content the right prominence. Promote a task to an activity if it is important and not currently being done correctly. Promote a business rule within a guide to a NOTE or task if it deserves more visibility.

Common questions

  • When to use a decision in a process flow? Reserve decision diamonds for critical decision points that impact the overall flow. The decision can lead to another process or a single alternate activity that terminates the process. For low-level decisions, use NOTEs or matrices instead.
  • How much detail to include? Consider audience, purpose, and don’t document everything. Document only details that add value. Assume the reader has the competency expected in that role.
  • Recommended maximum? Maximum 10 activities per process map; around 10 tasks or NOTEs per activity.
  • Note vs parallel activities? Use NOTEs for exceptions or variations (under 20% of the time). Use parallel activities for either/or conditions or simultaneous scenarios (50:50).

Source: Nintex Process Writing Techniques, Nintex Community.

How to write a process

A step-by-step guide to writing a process in line with CoVE Golden Rules, RMIT Tips and Nintex Techniques. Use it as a reference while you build your process in this tool, in Word, or in Nintex Process Manager.

Step 1 - Define the basics

Every process needs a clear identity before you start writing the steps. Capture these six fields first.

  • Process name - verb-first, 4-7 words. Example: "Create new TeachVE Hub site page".
  • Owner - the person accountable for the process. One named individual.
  • Expert - the subject-matter expert who can answer questions. Must differ from the owner.
  • Trigger - the event that starts the process. Example: "Staff member requests a new laptop via ServiceNow".
  • Inputs - what is needed to begin. Example: "Completed Facilities Request Form".
  • Outputs - what the process produces. Example: "Laptop provided to staff member".

Step 2 - Map your high-level activities first

CoVE Golden Rule 2: see the overall flow before adding detail. Most processes have 3-7 activities.

  • Number activities 1.0, 2.0, 3.0 … in the order they happen.
  • Start each activity title with a verb - "Receive", "Review", "Approve", "Notify", "Close".
  • Keep titles short - aim for 2-7 words. If a title is long, the activity is probably doing too much.
  • Don't pre-write the tasks - write the activity titles first to confirm the shape of the process.

Step 3 - Assign one role per activity

CoVE Golden Rule 3: one accountable role per activity. Other roles can contribute, but only one is accountable.

  • Use specific role titles, not "ALL STAFF" (which would put the process on every Promapp dashboard).
  • Use job titles or team names - not individual people's names.
  • If the role changes between two activities, plan an explicit handover (CoVE Rule 11): a "Notify…" or "Send…" task at the end of the previous activity.

Step 4 - Add the tasks under each activity

Tasks are the lettered steps a, b, c … under each activity. Keep them simple.

  • 1-10 tasks per activity is the sweet spot. More than 10 means the activity is too big.
  • Start each task with a verb. Examples: "Verify the request", "Create the user account", "Send the welcome email".
  • Use approved verbs. The tool checks against a list of around 250 approved verbs - if a verb is flagged, swap to a clearer alternative.
  • Keep tasks under 25 words - if longer, split into two tasks.
  • Australian English - the tool auto-corrects common American spellings (organize → organise, color → colour, authorize → authorise).

Step 5 - Cover the 80%, use NOTEs for the 20%

CoVE Golden Rule 4: write the process for what happens most of the time. Use NOTEs for the rare exceptions.

  • Map the most common path first - the "happy path".
  • For exceptions, errors, or rare scenarios - add a NOTE attached to the relevant activity.
  • NOTE titles should be questions: "What if the form is incomplete?", "How do I escalate?".
  • Use parallel activities instead of NOTEs only when there's a genuine 50:50 split, not for exceptions.

Step 6 - Decisions: Yes/No questions

Decisions are where the process branches. Keep them simple.

  • Phrase decisions as a Yes/No question: "Has approval been granted?", "Is the form complete?".
  • The "No" path should usually lead to one follow-up action and then end (e.g. "Notify the requester → End"). Don't over-complicate the unhappy path.
  • Avoid more than two decisions in a row - if you have a tree of decisions, the process is probably better as a knowledge article.

Step 7 - Review against the standards

Before publishing, run your process through this tool to check it against:

  • CoVE Process Writing Golden Rules (11 rules)
  • RMIT Tips for Process Editors & Champions (activity title length, owner/expert separation, audience terms, verb usage)
  • Nintex Process Writing Techniques (80/20, sub-process triggers, decisions vs notes, verb-first naming)

The tool will flag anything that breaks the rules. Fix the errors, review the warnings, and consider the suggestions.

Quick example

Process name: Onboard new staff member
Owner: HR Manager
Expert: HR Business Partner
Trigger: New starter contract signed
Inputs: Signed contract, role description
Outputs: Staff member fully onboarded

1.0 Receive new starter notification
Role: HR Manager
a. Receive notification from recruitment system
b. Verify start date and role
c. Create onboarding ticket in ServiceNow

2.0 Setup access and accounts
Role: IT Support Officer
a. Create user account
b. Configure permissions for their role
c. Notify the manager when access is ready
NOTE: What if access is delayed? Escalate to the IT lead and update the new starter.

How to import into Nintex Process Manager

New process:

  1. In Nintex Process Manager, click Create new process.
  2. Open the downloaded .txt file. Copy the process title (line 5) into the Nintex title field.
  3. Copy the Process Objective and Process Background blocks into their matching Nintex fields.
  4. Copy the Process Map block into the Nintex AI import / "Import process from text" field.
  5. Save and review.

Updating an existing process: archive the old version first, then either update links that reference it, or paste new content into the existing process shell to keep links intact. Don't leave two live versions in Nintex.
